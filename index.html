Clean Spaces
SF spends $60 per capita on street cleaning — 3× more than LA — yet 89% of streets show graffiti and 30% have feces. The budget grew 63% since 2018 with no measurable improvement in outcomes.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Garbage Infrastructure & Waste Containment:
Waste containment infrastructure has severe gaps: missing trash cans across multiple blocks, existing bins overflow without adequate emptying frequency, and illegal dumping costs $7.4M per year.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Bin Deserts & Inadequate Bin Coverage
SF removed 300–400 public trash cans in recent years, creating stretches of 2+ blocks without any receptacle, forcing people to leave trash on the sidewalk.
Without nearby bins, the sidewalk becomes the default disposal site. Removing bins in response to overflow complaints treats the symptom while worsening the cause.
Insufficient Bin Emptying Frequency
In high-need corridors, bins overflow between emptying cycles. The BLA audit found sweeping routes are not evaluated to optimize frequency or resource allocation.
Overflowing bins create a self-reinforcing degradation cycle: once loose waste accumulates, more people dump there. Current monitoring doesn't detect these problems in time.
Illegal Dumping of Bulk Items
Residents and businesses use public bins for furniture and debris, rapidly filling capacity meant for routine litter and creating hazardous accumulations in public space.
Illegal dumping degrades neighborhoods and consumes cleaning capacity. DPW has no written procedures and has gone 10 years without collecting $3M in outstanding fines.
People experiencing homelessness rummaging Through Trash Bins
Unhoused residents search public bins for food and recyclables, emptying contents onto adjacent sidewalks — turning a contained litter problem into a dispersed one.
Rummaging transforms contained litter into scattered waste, multiplying the cleaning burden and accelerating bin infrastructure deterioration across the city.
Operational Inefficiency and lack of processes
SF measures cleaning activities — hours worked, routes completed — but doesn't evaluate whether those activities produce clean streets. Performance goals have grown increasingly vague since 2015.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
High Spending with Poor Outcomes
SF spends $60 per capita on cleaning — 3× LA — but produces no better outcomes. The budget grew 63% since 2018 with no documented improvement in cleanliness metrics.
When high spending persistently fails to improve outcomes, it signals a systemic accountability failure. There is no evidence that DPW evaluates whether its spending is working.
Budget Underspending & Misallocation
In FY 2023-24, $15.2M in street cleaning funds went unspent and $11M+ was redirected to other purposes: APEC summit, street vending enforcement, storm response.
Underspending and misallocation represent a direct reduction in the cleaning capacity residents paid for and didn't receive. DPW rejected the BLA audit findings, which were upheld.
Output Metrics Instead of Outcome Accountability
SF Public Works reports cleaning activities but doesn't evaluate whether they produce clean streets. Goals are vague and SF reports every two months while peer cities report weekly.
An accountability system built around outputs rewards effort regardless of impact. Without outcome metrics by corridor, DPW has no systematic basis for improving operations.
Lack of written enforcement procedures
Weak enforcement against illegal dumping and failure to collect fines discourages compliance. DPW has no written procedures and hasn't pursued $3M in unpaid fines for a decade.
Lack of enforcement procedures encourages repeat violations, reduces deterrence, and deprives the program of cleaning funds. $3M in uncollected fines; citations declining since 2020.
Fecal Contamination & Public Sanitation
SF faces a sanitation crisis: 30% of streets had feces in FY2024, the Pit Stop program was drastically cut, and sidewalk samples detected dangerous human pathogens.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Encampment-related waste
Encampments generate trash, debris, and human waste in public spaces. Encampment cleaning costs ~$9M annually — 15% of the total street cleaning budget.
Encampments create biohazard risks and increase cleaning costs. Encampment cleaning represents 15% of the total street cleaning budget.
Insufficient number of accessible public restrooms
The absence of accessible public restrooms forces unhoused people to defecate on streets and alleys. The Pit Stop program was dramatically cut just as feces sightings are rising.
Lack of accessible restrooms increases pathogen exposure and creates inaccessible spaces. Each Pit Stop installation reduces 311 feces reports by an average of 12 per week within 500m.
Fecal Contamination as a Public Health Hazard
Feces on SF sidewalks contain confirmed human pathogens from direct sampling: E. coli, Shigella, norovirus, and Cryptosporidium, posing direct risk to residents and unhoused people.
Street-level fecal contamination is not an aesthetic problem — it is a public health crisis. 30% of surveyed streets had feces in FY2024, up sharply in Tenderloin, Mission, and Bernal Heights.
Graffiti & Visual Blight
89% of SF streets have graffiti in FY2024-25, averaging 23 instances per surveyed block — worse than the prior year. The free removal program issued ~900 notices in its first year.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Pervasive Graffiti Citywide
89% of SF streets showed some form of graffiti in FY2024-25, averaging 23 instances per surveyed street — an increase from 18 in the prior year.
Graffiti at near-universal coverage signals a breakdown of civic ownership of public space. It degrades neighborhoods visually and suppresses economic activity even when crime rates are low.
Welcoming & Accesible Spaces
SF's public spaces face barriers that prevent low-income families and immigrant communities from using them fully: infrastructure gaps, complex permitting, costs, and visible disorder.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Physical Infrastructure & Design Gaps
SF parks score 91% on general maintenance, but a well-maintained park is not the same as a usable park. There is $1B+ in deferred maintenance and serious ADA compliance gaps.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Park Space Inequity by Income & Race
Low-income residents have 57% less nearby park space than high-income residents. Parks in neighborhoods of color are half the size but serve 5× more people per acre.
Smaller, more crowded parks are functionally noisier and harder to use for families. This spatial inequity compounds other disadvantages: lower incomes, smaller living spaces, less private green space.
ADA Accessibility Gaps & Deferred Compliance
Several SF recreation centers are closed for ADA renovations with no published reopening timelines. These closures disproportionately affect low-income neighborhoods where it is the only community space.
When a family member uses a wheelchair or an elderly grandparent needs level paths, ADA non-compliance excludes that family entirely. The backlog exceeds $1B despite $380M+ invested through bonds.
Missing Basic Family Amenities
Many neighborhood parks in dense, low-income areas lack shade, seating, drinking fountains, accessible restrooms, and stroller-navigable pathways — basics without which a park is unusable.
A park without shade is unusable in heat for families with infants. Without a restroom, it can't be used for more than 30 minutes. POPOs (privately owned public spaces) are almost all in the Financial District.
Programming, Activation & Cultural Barriers
A well-maintained park that offers no relevant programming is a missed opportunity. Permitting barriers, costs, and language exclusions systematically shut out the communities that most need parks.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Complex Permitting Discourages Community Activations
Organizing a community event in a SF park requires navigating a fragmented process that can involve 6 agencies, four months of lead time, and multiple fees — an insurmountable barrier for informal community groups.
When the administrative burden is too high, community organizations simply don't apply for permits. Only well-resourced organizations can consistently activate park space.
Insufficient Culturally Relevant & Multilingual Programming
SF park programming is not consistently offered in languages other than English and doesn't reflect the cultural traditions of immigrant communities with 100,000+ Cantonese and hundreds of thousands of Spanish speakers.
When an immigrant family arrives at a park and finds everything in English and no culturally connected activities, the park communicates it is not for them. Cultural access is a precondition for real use.
Affordability & Economic Barriers to Participation
SF charges fees to reserve picnic areas, sports fields, and recreational programs. Fee waivers exist but are difficult to navigate, especially for low-income immigrant families.
When a low-income family must pay to use a public park, a public resource becomes semi-private. Waivers require knowing they exist, applying in English, and trusting the bureaucracy.
Safety Conditions That Deter Family Use
A park can score well on maintenance and still be avoided by families if there is visible drug use near playgrounds, insufficient lighting, or encampments.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Drug Use Near Playgrounds & Family Park Spaces
Open-air drug use in or adjacent to parks has made many spaces unsafe for families. A February 2025 operation at Jefferson Square involved DEA, National Guard, and 7 agencies — resulting in 90 arrests.
When drug use is visible near a playground, parents stop bringing their children. This functional exclusion falls disproportionately on low-income neighborhoods without alternative parks.
Insufficient Lighting in Public Spaces
Many SF parks have insufficient lighting after dark, reducing their use for working families who only have morning and evening windows — effectively cutting parks to half their size.
Dark parks after 6 PM are half-size parks for working families. Lighting is documented to reduce crime and fear of crime — a high-impact, low-cost intervention.
Governance, Accountability & Fragmented Management
SF park challenges are well-documented, but fragmented management across Rec & Park, Public Works, SFMTA, the Port, and the NPS creates incomplete feedback loops and unclear accountability.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Recreation Centers Closed for Renovations — No Published Timeline
Multiple recreation centers are closed for ADA renovations with no published reopening timelines. Closures affect low-income neighborhoods where the center is the only covered community space.
A recreation center closure is not a minor inconvenience — it is the loss of the neighborhood's only indoor community space for youth programs, senior activities, and meetings. Multi-year closures are invisible.
Fragmented Management & No Unified Community Feedback Mechanism
Management of SF public space is divided among multiple agencies with no unified interface for reporting problems, tracking outcomes, or participating in planning. 311 was not designed for this purpose.
When a family doesn't know which agency is responsible and there's no single place to report, problems go unresolved. Communities with the most at stake are least able to navigate a fragmented system.
Street and Traffic Safety
SF adopted Vision Zero in 2014 with a goal of zero deaths by 2024. Result: 363 dead, serious injuries +33%, and 2024 had the most transit deaths since 2005. Pedestrians are 56% of victims.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Dangerous Street Design:
The physical geometry of SF streets — high-speed one-way streets, wide intersections, long crossing distances — is the primary driver of traffic deaths, more than individual behavior.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
High Injury Network — Concentration of Danger
12% of SF streets concentrate 68% of severe and fatal traffic injuries. 44% of those miles are in equity priority communities — the most vulnerable with fewest resources to avoid them.
Concentrating danger on a known set of streets makes interventions manageable — but only if the city acts. As of 2023, ~50 miles of the High Injury Network still had no safety improvements.
One-Way Streets as Speed Infrastructure
SF's extensive one-way street network — built for suburban 20th-century traffic — enables dangerous speeds, aggressive left turns, and blind spots. One-way streets cause 3.8× more injuries.
One-way streets act as urban freeways through dense residential and commercial neighborhoods. Cities that convert them to two-way achieve 23–60% reductions in crashes.
Intersection Design Failures
Over 70% of injury collisions in SF occur at intersections, especially wide, high-speed ones with insufficient pedestrian protection and sight lines blocked by parked vehicles.
Intersection design failures are the most tractable and cost-effective category to address. Simple 'daylighting' measures reduced collisions 14% in the Tenderloin.
Speeding & Driver Behavior:
Excessive speed is the leading cause of serious injuries and deaths on SF streets. SF has the highest volume of speeding of any city measured by its camera contractor.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Pervasive Speeding on City Streets
Vehicles on SF streets routinely exceed speed limits lethally. Cameras issued 400,000+ warnings in early months — averaging 1,000+ per day across 33 locations.
Speed is the single most important factor in whether a crash is fatal. A pedestrian hit at 20 MPH has a 90% survival chance; at 40 MPH that drops to 40%.
Collapse of SFPD Traffic Enforcement
Traffic citations issued by SFPD fell nearly 90% from 2014 to 2024 — effectively eliminating traditional enforcement as a deterrent during the entire Vision Zero era.
The near-total collapse of SFPD traffic enforcement created a decade-long deterrence vacuum. A Civil Grand Jury explicitly blamed SFPD. Automated speed cameras launched in 2025 began closing the gap.
Hit-and-Run Deaths
Hit-and-run collisions — where drivers flee after striking pedestrians or cyclists — represented 27% of SF traffic deaths in 2023: 7 of 26 victims, primarily pedestrians.
Hit-and-run deaths are a compounded injustice: victims die in a preventable crash and then have no possibility of the driver being held accountable.
Vulnerable Road Users: Pedestrians, Seniors & Cyclists
The human cost of SF's traffic safety failure falls disproportionately on those least protected: pedestrians (56% of 2024 deaths), older adults (38% of 2023 victims), and cyclists.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Pedestrian Death & Injury Epidemic
24 of 43 traffic deaths in 2024 were pedestrians — 56% — despite being a small fraction of users. In 2023, all 6 female victims were pedestrians.
Pedestrians are the most physically vulnerable users. High speeds, wide streets, long crossing distances, and signal failures make walking in SF dangerous — more so in low-income neighborhoods.
Seniors as Disproportionate Victims
Adults 65+ are killed in SF traffic crashes at rates far exceeding their share of street use — 38% of victims in 2023. Their slower crossing speeds make them especially vulnerable.
As SF's population ages, the danger for seniors on streets becomes more urgent. Addressing their vulnerability requires specific design: more crossing time, reduced distances, refuge islands.
Cyclist Safety & Infrastructure Gaps
Despite significant progress on cycling safety during Vision Zero (injuries -35%, deaths ~0 from 2013–2023), cyclist deaths increased in 2024 and infrastructure gaps persist.
The cycling safety improvement from 2013 to 2023 is one of Vision Zero's genuine successes — but it is not permanent. The 2024 increase and infrastructure gaps show advances are fragile.
Safe Spaces
SF crime statistics show historic improvement — crime -25–30% in 2024, homicides at a 70-year low — but most residents don't feel it. The perception-reality gap is the central challenge.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Perception & Narrative: The Safety Doom Loop
SF has become a national symbol of progressive urban failure — a narrative amplified by viral videos, national political actors, and media that disproportionately covered the worst conditions.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Safety Perception Lags Dramatically Behind Crime Reality
Residents' subjective sense of safety has been significantly more negative than the objective data. In the year of SF's historic property crime low (2023), it recorded its worst safety rating since 1996.
When residents feel unsafe regardless of actual conditions, they change behavior — avoiding parks, staying home — creating the very conditions that make neighborhoods less safe.
Media & Political Actors Amplify Worst-Case Narratives
SF became a national reference for progressive urban failure: viral retail theft videos, open drug use, and national coverage that diverged from actual crime trends.
The 'doom loop' narrative had measurable economic consequences: dramatic reductions in office occupancy, consumer traffic, and tourism. The reputation for unsafety deterred investment and job recruitment.
Visible Urban Disorder: Drug Markets, Overdoses & Encampments
Even as measured crime falls, visible disorder — open drug use, public overdoses, encampments — signals loss of control of public space and suppresses community use.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Open-Air Drug Use & Markets in Public Spaces
Open-air drug markets in the Tenderloin and SoMa have allowed drug use to occur in plain sight — near BART stations, parks, and sidewalks. Markets migrate rather than close after enforcement.
Open-air drug use in public spaces creates an environment of visible suffering and perceived danger that deters residents, shoppers, tourists, and families from those spaces.
Homeless Encampments Near Parks & Public Spaces
Encampments adjacent to parks and plazas deter family use and create safety concerns. Under Mayor Lurie in 2025, encampment counts fell to their lowest since 2019.
The presence of encampments near parks creates a tension that neither forced enforcement nor ignoring them resolves well. Without adequate shelter capacity, clearing encampments moves people rather than houses them.
Actual Crime & Enforcement Gaps
Specific enforcement gaps continue to undermine street-level safety: SFPD operates 500+ officers below the recommended minimum, property crime clearance rates are 8.7%, and repeat offenders cycle through.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
SFPD Chronically Understaffed Citywide
SFPD has operated more than 500 officers below its recommended minimum staffing since 2020 (1,475 of 2,074). The department compensated primarily through overtime — unsustainable and costly.
Understaffing creates both real and perceived safety gaps. With 500+ vacancies, SFPD has less capacity for community policing, proactive enforcement, and drug market patrols.
Low Property Crime Clearance Rates
Despite falling property crime volumes, SFPD resolves only 8.7% of larceny theft cases. Most property crime victims receive no accountability outcome.
Low clearance rates erode trust in law enforcement regardless of whether crime volumes fall. A resident whose car is broken into for the third time doesn't experience 'crime is down.'
Short Pre-Trial Detention Enables Repeat Offending
Repeat drug offenders and prolific property criminals are frequently released days after arrest, due to Prop 47, judicial discretion, and limited jail and treatment capacity.
Repeat offending by a small number of prolific individuals drives a disproportionate share of visible street disorder and property crime, creating a visible revolving-door effect.
Accountability & Institutional Trust
The deepest challenge to public safety is not the crime rate, which genuinely improved, but whether residents believe the city can respond effectively and whether communities of color trust the police.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Residents Distrust the City's Safety Response — Unevenly Across Neighborhoods
Resident trust in the city's safety response is divided by neighborhood, race, income, and political affiliation. Low-income communities and communities of color have lower satisfaction with police.
Without trust in the safety response, residents under-report crime, avoid cooperating with police, and disinvest from public space. Areas with lowest trust have the highest need.
Weak Enforcement Culture & Low Prosecution Rates for Property Crime
For several years post-pandemic, a pattern emerged where SFPD didn't make arrests perceived as unprosecuted, and the DA declined cases at higher rates — a blame cycle that reduced effectiveness.
When enforcement and prosecution function poorly — officers not arresting, DA not filing cases, courts releasing detainees — the system loses credibility. 'Why call the police?' becomes a rational response.
Infrastructure Stewardship
SF's deferred maintenance backlog grows more expensive every year investment is delayed. DPW manages $19B in assets with a $600M+ backlog, while an $817M structural deficit forces more capital cuts.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Deferred Maintenance in Buildings, Streets & Sidewalks
SF's most tangible infrastructure challenge is an accumulated deferred maintenance backlog of $600M+, concentrated in public buildings, sidewalks, streets, and urban furniture managed by DPW.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Deferred Maintenance Backlog in Public Buildings & Infrastructure
DPW manages nearly $19B in public assets but does not publicly disclose comprehensive condition assessments or quantify the true cost of restoring degraded assets, per the 2024 Civil Grand Jury.
Deferred maintenance is not benign neglect — it is cost acceleration. A pothole becomes a sinkhole; a leaking roof becomes a structural failure. Deteriorated assets create direct safety risks.
25-Year Sidewalk Inspection Cycle Creates Sustained Safety Hazards
SF's Sidewalk Inspection and Repair Program operates on a 25-year cycle — any given block may go more than two decades without an official inspection. Tree root damage accumulates between inspections.
Damaged sidewalks are a direct safety hazard: trip-and-fall injuries result in significant legal liability. The $1.4B Willits ADA settlement indicates the 25-year cycle is inadequate.
Seismic & Climate Risk
The Embarcadero Seawall is SF's most consequential infrastructure risk: 72% probability of M6.7+ earthquake before 2043, potential $30B in damages, and protecting $100B+ in assets and critical utility services.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Embarcadero Seawall: Earthquake Vulnerability of Critical Regional Infrastructure
The 100-year-old Embarcadero Seawall is at significant risk of damage or collapse in a major earthquake. It was built before seismic engineering standards existed and sits on liquefiable Bay mud.
Seawall failure during an earthquake is not just a Port problem — it is a citywide emergency response problem. It would damage utility lines, flood BART/Muni tunnels, and isolate downtown.
Sea Level Rise & Climate Risk to Coastal & Utility Infrastructure
The Seawall's vulnerability is not limited to earthquakes. Sea level has already risen 8 inches since 1900 and is projected to rise up to 5 feet by 2100, with extreme tides already overtopping the wall.
Climate risk to infrastructure is a compounding problem: the same assets facing seismic risk also face sea level rise risk. Budget cuts reduce investment precisely when the climate crisis demands more.
Aging Utility Systems: Water, Sewer & Power
Below SF's streets runs an aging network of water mains, sewers, and power conduits that forms the city's basic services infrastructure. Parts are up to 100 years old.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Aging Water & Sewer Infrastructure With Accelerating Replacement Costs
SF's water and sewer systems include infrastructure more than 100 years old. The SFPUC's capital plan grew 34% in one year — from $8.8B to $11.8B — driven by aging assets, regulations, and climate change.
Aging water and sewer infrastructure creates risks across multiple dimensions: public health, environmental compliance, seismic vulnerability, and escalating costs from continued deferral.
Governance, Transparency & Fiscal Stewardship
SF's infrastructure governance is fragmented across six major agencies with different funding cycles, making long-term asset maintenance accountability difficult.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
DPW Lacks Public Asset Accounting & Independent Oversight
DPW manages approximately $19B in assets without disclosing comprehensive condition assessments. The Public Works Commission, created for independent oversight, does not yet have defined powers.
Without comprehensive asset condition accounting, rational prioritization of maintenance investment is impossible. The Civil Grand Jury found even DPW leadership lacks full visibility into the backlog.
Structural Budget Deficit Systematically Crowds Out Capital Investment
SF's structural deficit of $817.5M for FY2025-26 creates sustained pressure to cut capital programs. DPW's budget was already cut 5.4% ($24.4M) with reductions in capital programs and infrastructure.
The fiscal dynamic is perverse: the deficit forces capital cuts now, which increases the deferred maintenance backlog, which increases future repair costs. A debt that compounds while the problem grows.
🔵 Transform Our Health and Homelessness System

Challenge DimensionSummaryActions
Breaking the Cycle (Homelessness)
At any given time, 8,323 people experience homelessness in SF (4,354 unsheltered). For every person who exits homelessness, ~3 new people enter the system. The city spends ~$690M/year without resolving the imbalance.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Structural/Systemic Issues
SF's homelessness crisis has structural causes: decades of restrictive zoning, the most expensive housing market in the country, systemic racial inequality, and a system built to manage — not end — homelessness.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Housing Unaffordability and Supply Shortage
SF has one of the most expensive housing markets in the world. Median price ~$1.29M; average rent $3,148/month — 55% above the national average. The shortage of affordable supply means any personal crisis can trigger homelessness.
When housing costs vastly exceed incomes, any life disruption can push people into homelessness. Without affordable alternatives, displaced residents have nowhere to go.
Racial Inequality and Systemic Disparities
Homelessness in SF is deeply racialized. Black residents are ~5% of the population but 25–37% of the homeless population; Hispanic/Latino residents 15% of the population but 34% of those experiencing homelessness.
Communities historically excluded from housing access and wealth accumulation face greater vulnerability to homelessness. Without addressing racial equity, interventions will continue to fail the most marginalized groups.
Poverty, Income Inequality, and Cost of Living
SF's extreme income inequality — where tech wealth coexists with deep poverty — produced a housing market structurally inaccessible to low-income residents. Median income of those who lose housing: $960/month.
When housing costs are so far beyond the reach of low-income residents that no income or subsidy can bridge the gap, homelessness becomes the structural endpoint of poverty.
High Inflow Rate and Inadequate Prevention
SF's homelessness response system is overwhelmed by the rate of new entries. Prevention programs — rental assistance, eviction defense, financial aid — are severely underfunded relative to need.
When 3 people enter homelessness for every 1 who exits, the overall population cannot decrease regardless of how effective exit programs are. Prevention is the highest-leverage, lowest-cost intervention.
Behavioral / Health System
A large share of SF's homeless population lives with untreated or inadequately treated mental illness. The city needs 152–225 new psychiatric beds; staff shortages reduced capacity 15–20% in 2023.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Substance Use and the Fentanyl/Drug Crisis
SF faces a severe intersection of homelessness and addiction, compounded by the fentanyl epidemic. Critically, research shows most drug use among homeless individuals begins *after* losing housing.
Substance use disorders make it harder to find and maintain employment and housing. But since homelessness itself drives drug adoption, treating addiction without addressing housing instability won't resolve the crisis.
Mental Health Crisis and Inadequate Behavioral Health System
A large and growing share of SF's homeless population lives with untreated mental illness. Without adequate treatment, people with psychiatric conditions and homelessness cannot stabilize.
Without adequate mental health treatment, individuals with psychiatric conditions and homelessness cannot stabilize. Insufficient capacity creates a costly revolving door between hospitals, jails, and streets.
Reducing Health Disparities
SF's 83-year life expectancy and near-universal insurance coverage mask some of the most severe racial health disparities in the country. Black, Latino, and Pacific Islander residents fare worse on virtually every metric.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Social Determinants of Health
SF's health disparities are driven less by individual behavior than by daily living conditions: poverty, residential segregation, unequal access to healthy food, and neighborhood environment.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Income & Wealth Gap as Health Driver
Extreme income inequality between racial groups in SF means Black, Latino, and Pacific Islander residents systematically lack resources to access health-promoting goods.
Income predicts life expectancy more than any other single factor. Black SF households earn $47K vs. $158K for white households — a 3.4:1 ratio that creates compounding barriers to health.
Food Deserts & Nutrition Insecurity
Several SF neighborhoods with the highest concentrations of low-income residents of color are food deserts — lacking affordable fresh produce — directly linked to diabetes and cardiovascular disease.
Poor nutrition is directly linked to higher rates of obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and adverse birth outcomes — all conditions where racial disparities in SF are most severe.
Residential Segregation & Neighborhood Deprivation
Historical policies (redlining, Fillmore urban renewal, exclusionary zoning) concentrated poverty, pollution, and disinvestment in specific neighborhoods disproportionately home to communities of color.
Neighborhood conditions are a primary driver of health. Residents of low-income SF zip codes face higher hospitalization rates for diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease.
Prenatal, Maternal & Infant Health Disparities
SF has the worst birth disparities by race of any city in California. Black mothers have preterm birth rates of 16% (2024) — the highest since the state began tracking in 2007. The gap is widening.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Racial Gap in Preterm Birth & Infant Mortality
Black mothers in SF experience dramatically higher rates of preterm birth, very preterm birth, and infant mortality than white mothers — and the gap is widening, not narrowing.
Preterm birth is the leading cause of infant death and a major driver of lifelong health complications. Widening racial disparities mean outcomes are actively deteriorating for Black families.
Late / Absent Prenatal Care
Black and Latino mothers are significantly more likely to begin prenatal care late or not receive it — driven by financial, geographic, cultural, and systemic barriers.
Adequate prenatal care is the single most effective intervention to prevent preterm birth and maternal mortality. Late or absent care means risk factors go undetected and unmanaged.
Underspending on Maternal Equity Programs
Despite documented and worsening disparities, SF has chronically underspent its state maternal equity funding — returning millions to Sacramento while Black birth outcomes deteriorated.
Program underspending directly translates into fewer prenatal visits, fewer doulas for Black mothers, delayed home visits, and reduced culturally congruent care.
Chronic Disease Disparities
Chronic diseases — diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease — are SF's leading causes of death and affect Black and Latino residents at rates 3–7× higher than white residents.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Diabetes Hospitalization & Mortality Gap
Black and Latino SF residents face dramatically higher rates of type 2 diabetes diagnosis, hospitalization, and death. The hospitalization rate for Black residents is 6.7× that of white residents.
Diabetes is a disease of manageable risk — early diagnosis, lifestyle support, and medication access can prevent hospitalization and death. The racial gap reflects a failure of preventive care, not biological inevitability.
Cardiovascular Disease & Hypertension
Black SF residents are hospitalized for hypertension and heart failure at 3–5× the rate of all other racial groups — the highest documented cardiovascular disparity in the country.
Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in SF. Black residents in their 40s and 50s are hospitalized for heart disease at rates comparable to people of other races over 75 — a 25–35-year premature aging effect.
Overdose Death Disparities
SF's overdose crisis falls disproportionately on Black residents — particularly older Black men with histories of homelessness and economic marginalization — with a mortality rate 5× the citywide rate.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Black SF Residents' Extreme Overdose Mortality Rate
Black SF residents die from overdoses at catastrophically disproportionate rates: 33% of all overdose deaths in SF but only ~5% of the population. SF's Black overdose disparity exceeds any other major U.S. city.
This is not a drug-use prevalence disparity but a mortality disparity driven by economic precarity, housing instability, polysubstance exposure, and barriers to treatment.
Latinx Overdose Mortality — Emerging & Underserved Crisis
While Black residents face the most severe overdose disparity, the Latino community is experiencing the fastest-growing overdose mortality trend — including the first month on record where Latino deaths exceeded Black deaths.
Latino overdose deaths are growing faster than community-specific interventions are being built. Risk is compounded by undocumented status, language barriers in treatment settings, and social stigma.
Treatment Access Gaps & Cultural Mismatch
Even when Black and Latino SF residents want treatment for substance use disorders, they face barriers: culturally mismatched programs, geographic inaccessibility, and lack of peer navigators.
A disparity in overdose death rates driven by treatment access barriers is a solvable problem — but only if solutions are community-designed and culturally congruent.
Service Response: Cultural & Linguistic Barriers
Even where health services exist, they fail to reach the highest-disparity communities. Language, cultural, and trust barriers result in delayed preventive care and worse treatment outcomes.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Cultural & Linguistic Barriers in Clinical Care
Health services in SF are often delivered in ways that don't meet the cultural, linguistic, or trust needs of Black, Latino, and Pacific Islander communities.
Cultural competence is not an add-on — it is evidence-based care. Culturally congruent doulas, peer navigators, and community health workers improve birth outcomes, treatment engagement, and chronic disease management.
Implicit Bias & Discrimination in Clinical Settings
Implicit racial bias among healthcare providers — affecting how pain is assessed and concerns are taken seriously — contributes directly to worse outcomes for Black and Latino patients, particularly in maternal care.
Implicit bias is a documented cause of preventable maternal deaths. Black women's concerns about pain and complications are statistically less likely to be taken seriously by providers.
🟢 Keep Our City Safe
2 dimensions 17 issues
▶
Challenge DimensionSummaryActions
Open-Air Drug Markets & Public Drug Use
SF's open-air drug markets — concentrated in the Tenderloin and SoMa — represent the city's most visible public safety challenge. In 2025, 624 accidental overdose deaths were recorded, over 70% attributed to fentanyl.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Organized Drug Supply & Trafficking Networks
Open-air markets exist because street-level drug dealing is highly profitable and the supply chain is sophisticated. The Sinaloa Cartel manufactures fentanyl; Honduran organizations control Tenderloin street sales.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Transnational Drug Supply Chain
Fentanyl and other drugs are manufactured internationally and distributed through organized trafficking networks. Prices of $5–10/dose mean demand cannot be suppressed by arrests alone.
A sophisticated criminal supply chain delivers drugs at street prices so low that demand cannot be suppressed by arrests. The market constantly replenishes after enforcement operations.
Weak Deterrence / Short Jail Stays
Despite thousands of arrests, drug dealers return to the street within days due to average jail stays of 4–5 days and low prosecution rates. The business calculus for street dealing remains favorable.
Without meaningful deterrent consequences, the calculus for street dealing remains favorable. Dealers know they will be released in days — only ~3% of arrests result in post-conviction jail time.
Public Drug Use & Addiction Crisis
Public drug use persists because tens of thousands of people with severe substance use disorders lack timely access to treatment. 28% of homeless drug users wanted treatment but couldn't access it.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Fentanyl Overdose Epidemic
Fentanyl — ~50× more potent than heroin — has flooded the illicit drug supply since ~2019, making overdose highly likely even for experienced users. 624 accidental deaths in 2025.
Fentanyl's extreme potency means a microscopic dosing error is fatal. Deaths cluster in open-use areas like the Tenderloin, traumatizing residents and occurring in public view.
Insufficient Addiction Treatment Capacity
The number of people with severe substance use disorder in SF far exceeds available treatment resources. Only 46 spaces in city-run treatment centers; wait lists are common.
Treatment gaps force people with addiction to remain in open-air drug markets — as users and as participants in drug economies — because there is no viable alternative.
Drug-Related Violence & Assault
Open-air drug markets are associated with firearms, turf conflicts, armed robbery of drug users, and dealer-on-dealer violence, generating a persistent cycle of violent crime in affected neighborhoods.
The Tenderloin is consistently SF's highest-violence district relative to its size. Drug dealing inherently generates violence through competition, debt collection, and robbery of vulnerable users.
Enforcement & Response Systems
Even coordinated enforcement like DMACC cannot permanently eliminate markets when SFPD is critically understaffed, treatment capacity is insufficient, and dealers return in days.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
SFPD Officer Shortage
SF's police force is significantly understaffed (500+ vacancies), limiting its ability to maintain continuous enforcement pressure on drug markets, especially at night.
Without sustained 24/7 enforcement presence, drug markets reconstitute after daytime operations end. DMACC analysis showed drug arrests plummeted after midnight.
Drug Market Displacement
Intensive enforcement in one location — such as the Tenderloin — pushes drug markets to adjacent areas (Mission, SoMa, Civic Center) rather than eliminating them.
Displacement undermines enforcement ROI and extends the harms of open drug use to new neighborhoods, increasing the burden on already stretched police resources.
Nighttime Enforcement Gap
DMACC operations historically peaked during daytime hours with enforcement near-absent after midnight, creating predictable windows during which drug markets operated freely.
Drug dealing and use that migrates to nighttime hours enables markets to persist even under heavy daytime enforcement, prolonging community harm and making progress harder to measure.
Community & Economic Impact
The persistence of open-air drug markets creates tangible, measurable harms for people who live, work, and visit those neighborhoods: fear, exclusion from public spaces, business closures, and lost income.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Resident & Worker Safety Fears
Residents, workers, and visitors in drug market neighborhoods — especially low-income families and immigrants — experience daily exposure to drug use, violence, and criminal activity.
Living near open-air drug markets has documented psychological, physical, and economic effects. Families avoid streets, businesses lose staff and customers, and vulnerable residents face direct exposure to needles.
Economic & Business Harm
The concentration of open-air drug markets in central SF neighborhoods has driven away businesses, deterred investment, and suppressed downtown economic recovery post-COVID.
Business closures reduce tax revenues, eliminate jobs in affected neighborhoods, and create feedback loops where vacancy increases fear and attracts more drug market activity.
Retail Theft & Fencing of Stolen Goods
SF's retail theft crisis spans from individual shoplifting to organized smash-and-grab operations. Since 2023, enforcement and legislative reform produced measurable improvements, but structural vulnerabilities persist.
Problem AreaSummaryActions
Organized Retail Crime (ORC) Networks
Retail theft in SF is not primarily opportunistic shoplifting — it is driven by organized criminal networks that coordinate teams, use getaway vehicles, target specific stores repeatedly, and quickly convert theft to cash.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Coordinated Smash-and-Grab & Group Shoplifting
Criminal organizations deploy coordinated teams to rapidly strip merchandise, overwhelming store security before police respond. Organized retail theft incidents increased 29% from 2020 to 2022.
Individual employees cannot safely intervene; security costs become prohibitive. High-profile incidents at Neiman Marcus, Target, and Walgreens generated national coverage that damaged SF's reputation.
Recidivist / Repeat Offenders
A small number of prolific serial offenders account for a disproportionate share of all retail theft incidents. The top 10% of offenders represent 68% of the total value of theft events.
Without felony-level consequences for repeat offenders, theft becomes a low-risk business. Prop 36 (Nov 2024, 69% approval) reinstates felony charges for repeat thieves.
Fencing Networks & the Stolen Goods Economy
Fencing is the economic engine of retail theft: without markets to convert stolen merchandise into cash, the incentive to steal drops dramatically. The fencing network makes theft a viable business.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Open-Air Street Fencing Markets
Stolen goods are sold openly in street vendor markets along Mission Street, UN Plaza, and other corridors, providing near-immediate cash conversion for shoplifters.
Open-air fencing creates a visible, accessible market that directly incentivizes theft by guaranteeing quick cash for stolen goods — while degrading neighborhoods and displacing legitimate vendors.
Online Marketplace Fencing
Organized theft rings use major e-commerce platforms (eBay, Amazon, Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace) to resell stolen SF goods nationally at scale.
Online fencing dramatically expands the market for stolen goods beyond local buyers, makes theft more profitable, and allows criminal networks to operate geographically separate from the crime.
Economic & Community Impact
Retail theft's most lasting harm is the economic devastation of SF's commercial districts: Union Square's vacancy rate reached a record 20.6% in Q1 2024 — more than double the national average.
IssueDescriptionWhy it's a problem
Retail Business Exodus & Downtown Vacancy
High-profile chain store closures — five Walgreens, two Targets, Nordstrom, Macy's — accelerated a downtown 'doom loop,' pushing Union Square vacancy to a record 20.6% in Q1 2024.
Empty storefronts create physical and psychological blight: they deter shoppers, reduce foot traffic that sustains adjacent businesses, cut city tax revenues, and signal to retailers that SF is not viable.
Worker Safety & Security Cost Escalation
Retail workers — particularly in pharmacy chains, grocery stores, and convenience stores — face escalating risks of confrontation, assault, and intimidation from shoplifters and organized theft teams.
Worker safety concerns force retailers to bear enormous security costs, reduce hours, lock up merchandise — degrading the customer experience — or exit the market entirely.
Perception Damage & Tourism Deterrence
Viral social media videos and national news coverage of SF retail theft created lasting reputational damage that deterred shoppers, tourists, and potential tenants.
Perception lags reality in urban retail recovery. The reputational harm from 2021–2022 incidents continued suppressing Union Square foot traffic and visitor spending into 2024.
